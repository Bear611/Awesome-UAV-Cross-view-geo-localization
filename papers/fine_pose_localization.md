| [Visual Orientation Posterior for Post-Retrieval UAV Geo-Localization](https://doi.org/10.1109/CAIT70489.2026.11553955) | Cross-view UAV geo-localization is typically framed as image retrieval, yet a correct top-1 match does not guarantee an accurate final pose. This paper addresses that gap by proposing a lightweight post-retrieval verification framework built around a Visual Orientation Posterior (VOP) module. VOP predicts a discrete posterior over candidate camera orientations, and the system uses this distribution to allocate sparse geometric verification only to a small subset of geometry-promising orientation hypotheses rather than exhaustively testing all possibilities. This sparse design keeps memory usage and inference latency low enough for onboard deployment on small and medium UAVs whose edge processors operate under tight resource budgets, where dense matching methods are impractical. The verification head is trained with supervision derived from final localization quality, so the selected hypotheses are explicitly aligned with downstream geometric accuracy. The authors evaluate the approach on the GTA-UAV and UAV-VisLoc benchmarks, reporting consistent improvements over sparse and rotation-enumeration baselines as well as LoFTR on the proposed final localization protocol, and a narrowed gap to dense DKM while remaining much more efficient, confirming suitability for resource-constrained aerial platforms. | GTA-UAV, UAV-VisLoc | - |

The authors propose NavCLIP, a two-stage vision-based pipeline that localizes a UAV by matching onboard nadir-facing aerial imagery against a tiled satellite image database. The localization module builds on a GeoCLIP-inspired CLIP-style architecture, pairing a frozen ViT-L/14 image encoder with a multi-scale Gaussian Random Fourier Feature location encoder, and aligns cross-view embeddings through contrastive learning to retrieve the best-matching satellite tile. The orientation module then applies MapGlue for detector-free keypoint correspondence between the retrieved tile and the query aerial image, and estimates a homography via RANSAC to recover both the UAV position and yaw angle. For training and evaluation the authors construct a new paired UAV-Satellite Dataset collected with a DJI Mavic Air 2 over the NTU campus, together with a Google Earth benchmark covering NTU campus, Taichung Station, and a cross-season UCLA zero-shot test set. On the controlled Google Earth benchmark, NavCLIP achieves around 1.5-pixel localization errors (≈1.21 pixels / 1.17 m on the cross-season UCLA split) and a mean heading error of 0.2°, substantially outperforming the GeoCLIP baseline.
